Reducing friction, amplifying understanding — Kakao Work attendance redesign.

Attendance management that anyone can start right from the first screen.

Kakao Work's attendance module had a serious onboarding problem. HR managers spent days configuring rules before employees could clock a single shift. Employees found the interface confusing enough that help desk tickets about basic attendance functions were among the top support categories.

The system wasn't broken — it was built for power users and then handed to everyone else. The challenge was designing a zero learning curve experience without stripping away the configurability enterprise clients depended on.

We had to make it feel effortless on day one, without making it brittle on day one thousand.

My Role

I led end-to-end design from concept to deployment — owning the full product design process across research, architecture, interaction design, and handoff. This was one of the most operationally complex projects of my career: attendance policy logic is genuinely intricate, and the surface area of configuration options required deep collaboration with the PM and engineering teams to understand what was actually possible.

Working within Kakao Enterprise's design system, I simultaneously had to advocate for simplification while preserving the depth that large enterprise clients required. That tension — between simplicity and power — defined every major design decision on this project.

Research
Zero Learning Curve UX Design

The first screen is a promise. We had to keep it.

Through bi-weekly usability testing and field research with HR managers and employees, we identified the core issue: the mental model gap. Users thought in terms of outcomes (I want everyone on a 9-to-6 schedule, with 15 days of annual leave) — but the interface was organized around configurations (define shift rules, assign policy groups, configure exception handling).

What research revealed:

HR managers consistently described their setup experience as "having to think like a developer." Employees described the daily attendance UI as "I just click what I think is right and hope it worked." Both groups were experiencing the same root problem: the system's internal logic was leaking into the surface design.

We restructured the information architecture around two core jobs-to-be-done:

For employees: Clock in, clock out, check my leave balance, request time off. Four actions. These should take one tap each.

For administrators: Set up a policy, assign it to a team, handle exceptions. Three phases. Each phase should feel like a guided workflow, not a configuration panel.

Design
One-click templates. Intuitive UX writing. Continuous refinement.

The insight that changed everything: templates eliminate the blank page problem.

The highest drop-off in the admin setup flow happened at the very first configuration screen — a blank policy editor. Administrators stared at it, unsure where to start, and either called support or abandoned the setup entirely.

"One-click templates enable employees to track attendance and vacation days instantly, while intuitive UX writing empowers administrators to configure settings tailored to their company's unique needs."

We introduced a policy template library covering the most common workforce structures — standard 9-to-5, flexible hours, shift-based schedules, remote-first policies. Administrators could start from a template that matched their organization's shape and customize from there, rather than building from scratch.

The second major intervention was UX writing. Every label, tooltip, and confirmation message was rewritten with the administrator's mental model in mind. Instead of "Define attendance rule parameters," we wrote "When does your team's workday start?" Instead of "Configure exception policy," we wrote "What should happen when someone forgets to clock in?"

Through iterative CBT testing — running structured tests with real HR managers and employees at multiple points in the design process — we continuously validated and refined both interventions until the drop-off rate at first setup fell to near zero.

After launch, routine task completion time dropped by the equivalent of 3–5 clicks per action — translating to hundreds of hours saved across enterprise clients monthly.
